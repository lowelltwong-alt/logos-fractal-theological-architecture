# GraphRAG Needs for Biblical and Theological Connection Vocabulary

## Problem

Generic graph relations are not enough for biblical studies or theology.

A standard GraphRAG graph can answer basic questions such as what is connected to this node, what is cited by this document, and what themes co-occur. But biblical and theological interpretation needs more disciplined distinctions.

## What standard graphs often lack

### 1. Relationship directness

The graph must distinguish direct quotation, explicit citation, probable allusion, weak echo, shared motif, typological correspondence, later reception, and AI-inferred similarity.

### 2. Hermeneutical method

The graph needs to know whether a connection depends on grammar, literary context, historical context, canonical reading, typology, reception history, redemptive-historical reading, archaeological contextualization, lexical semantics, or textual criticism.

Without method metadata, AI will render all relations as if they are the same kind of evidence.

### 3. Authority scope

The graph must distinguish Scripture, creed, confession, doctrine node, commentary, academic hypothesis, tradition-specific interpretation, application bridge, AI-generated summary, and inferred retrieval relation.

Without authority scope, retrieval can flatten source hierarchy.

### 4. Tradition scope

The graph must expose when a relation is shared Christian core, Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Protestant, Reformed, Lutheran, Anglican, Jewish, rabbinic, Second Temple, patristic, academic-critical, or contested across traditions.

### 5. Evidence type

The graph must distinguish textual evidence, lexical evidence, manuscript evidence, translation evidence, archaeological evidence, historical evidence, theological argument, reception history, and application reasoning.

### 6. Asserted vs inferred status

A relation should say whether it is directly asserted by a source, proposed by a scholar, inferred by a rule, inferred by AI retrieval, generated by summarization, contested, or rejected.

## Required AI rendering fields

Every serious biblical/theological graph relation should eventually expose relationship_type, scholarly_label, source_id, target_id, assertion_mode, evidence_type, source_basis, authority_scope, tradition_scope, hermeneutical_method, confidence, review_status, ai_rendering_rule, and misuse_risk.

## Route-specific rendering needs

### Short answer route

Must avoid overstating weak connections.

Example rendering rule: this is an allusion proposed by a source, not a direct quotation.

### Evidence bundle route

Must show source, method, and confidence.

Example rendering rule: this fulfillment claim depends on a typological reading, not a direct prediction.

### Graph neighborhood route

Must group by relationship family: textual structure, quotation / allusion / echo, typology, prophecy / fulfillment, lexical / semantic, witness / textual criticism, archaeology / context, doctrine / application.

### Review-ready diff route

Must show whether a PR adds new theological meaning, not only new edges.

## Anti-drift rules

1. Do not use `related_to` for theologically meaningful relations.
2. Do not use `fulfills` without specifying fulfillment model.
3. Do not use `quotes` when the relation is allusion or echo.
4. Do not use archaeology relations as doctrinal proof.
5. Do not use translation relations as original-language meaning without support.
6. Do not hide whether a relationship is asserted, inferred, or AI-generated.

## Recommended first graph subset

Start small: quotes, alludes_to, echoes, typologically_corresponds_to, claims_fulfillment_of, translated_as, has_lemma, has_reading, attested_by_witness, interpreted_by, archaeologically_contextualized_by, grounds_doctrine, and constrains_application.
