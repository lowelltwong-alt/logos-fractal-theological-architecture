# Truth, Deception, and Moral Authority

Author: Lowell T. Wong  
Status: draft  
Node type: doctrine  
Canonical ID: doctrine.truth.deception.moral_authority

## Summary

Truth is not created by usefulness or model fluency. Christians should evaluate AI in light of whether it serves truth, obscures truth, or normalizes deception.

## Why this doctrine matters for Christian AI discernment

AI systems can generate plausible outputs, synthetic personas, persuasive language, fabricated imagery, and simulated relational presence. This doctrine asks whether usefulness is being allowed to outrank truthfulness.

## Core claim

An AI system should not be treated as morally acceptable simply because it is effective. Christians must ask whether it produces, rewards, hides, or normalizes deception.

## Plain-language meaning

In simple terms:
- something sounding convincing does not make it true
- fake relational presence is not morally neutral
- hidden automation can become deceptive
- people remain responsible for truth even when a tool makes lying easier

## AI implications

### Stable implications across most Christian paths
- undisclosed impersonation is morally suspect
- fabricated citations, invented facts, and false witnesses are serious concerns
- synthetic intimacy or simulated spirituality may create truth confusion
- AI systems should be judged partly by how they shape honesty and trust

### Questions this doctrine raises
- is this system truthful about what it is?
- does it increase confusion between real and simulated persons?
- does it make deception easier, cheaper, or more socially normal?
- are users being manipulated through realistic but false outputs?

## Related AI issues
- AI companions and romantic chatbots
- deepfakes and synthetic media
- AI preaching or devotional content without disclosure
- AI-generated school or workplace fraud
- AI persuasion tools
