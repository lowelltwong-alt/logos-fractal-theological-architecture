# Christian Counseling Side-Quest Roadmap

## Purpose

This note preserves a possible future branch for connecting the Logos architecture to Christian mental health counselors, pastoral-care leaders, theologians, and AI / ontology contributors.

It is intentionally a side-quest roadmap, not an active build plan.

The purpose is to keep the idea explicit while preventing premature clinical, pastoral, or AI-counseling work from going off the rails.

## Current status

Do not build this domain yet.

Current posture:

- planning only;
- no counseling content;
- no client-specific guidance;
- no AI counselor product;
- no clinical diagnosis, treatment, or triage workflows;
- no intake forms, therapy-note handling, or client-record ingestion;
- no contributor launch to counselors until safety boundaries exist.

## Why this might matter later

Christian counseling could become a high-value application area because it sits at the intersection of:

- theological anthropology;
- imago Dei and human dignity;
- fallenness, shame, suffering, and hope;
- pastoral care and discipleship;
- trauma-informed care;
- mental health ethics;
- licensed clinical practice;
- church referral networks;
- AI safety and role-boundary governance.

The Logos repository could eventually help Christian counselors and pastoral-care teams build structured, reviewable resources without flattening theology, clinical care, pastoral care, and AI support into one unsafe category.

## Non-negotiable boundary

This future branch must not become an AI therapy bot.

The safe future version is a governed knowledge and contributor pathway that helps qualified people structure theological, pastoral, clinical-boundary, and referral knowledge.

It must not provide individualized diagnosis, treatment planning, crisis triage, therapy, or client-specific counseling advice.

## Future contributor lanes

If this branch is activated later, contributors should be separated by lane.

### Licensed Christian mental health professionals

Potential role:

- clinical ethics review;
- scope-of-practice boundaries;
- safety and escalation guidance;
- intervention-status review;
- professional terminology review.

### Pastors, chaplains, and pastoral-care leaders

Potential role:

- pastoral-care boundaries;
- discipleship, prayer, lament, confession, forgiveness, and referral pathways;
- care-team workflow cautions;
- church-role distinctions.

### Theologians and biblical scholars

Potential role:

- theology of personhood;
- sin, suffering, shame, lament, hope, healing, sanctification, and embodied care;
- Scripture-use boundaries;
- doctrine-to-care bridges.

### Trauma and abuse-informed reviewers

Potential role:

- trauma-informed cautions;
- abuse and coercion boundaries;
- language that avoids spiritual bypassing, minimization, or blame;
- escalation requirements.

### AI / ontology contributors

Potential role:

- metadata structure;
- contributor workflow;
- validation rules;
- graph and claim-object design;
- retrieval and rendering safety.

They should not author clinical claims without qualified review.

## Who should not contribute directly to this domain

Do not allow this domain to be shaped by contributors who are trying to:

- provide therapy without credentials;
- upload client stories, therapy notes, or identifiable case material;
- turn theological claims into one-size-fits-all treatment protocols;
- frame mental illness as only spiritual failure;
- bypass medical or clinical care where it is needed;
- write crisis guidance without licensed professional review;
- use the project to promote fringe or coercive practices;
- replace pastoral or clinical accountability with AI output.

## Future build phases

### Phase 0: Parking-lot status

Current phase.

Keep this as a remembered possible future branch. Do not add active counseling content.

### Phase 1: Safety and role-boundary seed

Create a staged research packet that defines:

- contributor role lanes;
- scope-of-practice boundaries;
- crisis escalation boundaries;
- privacy and client-data prohibitions;
- pastoral-care vs clinical-care distinctions;
- AI use and non-use rules;
- review requirements before any counseling-adjacent material is promoted.

This should be staged as incoming research and not promoted directly into application docs.

### Phase 2: Domain-specific front door

Create a future Christian counseling front door after the safety seed exists.

It should explain:

- what the Logos repo is;
- what this domain could become;
- what qualified counselors can contribute;
- what pastors and theologians can contribute;
- what AI contributors can and cannot do;
- how to stage safe material;
- what must never be added;
- how professional review works.

### Phase 3: Vocabulary and safety taxonomy

Create a controlled vocabulary seed for:

- contributor roles;
- care settings;
- care postures;
- theological domains;
- clinical domains;
- pastoral-care domains;
- crisis boundaries;
- privacy boundaries;
- evidence types;
- intervention status;
- review status.

### Phase 4: First draft bridge pack

Only after Phases 1-3 should the repo create draft application bridges, such as:

- theological anthropology and client dignity;
- suffering, lament, and care boundaries;
- fallenness, shame, and non-shaming care;
- hope, healing, and referral boundaries;
- pastoral-care and licensed-care distinction.

These bridge files should be educational and governance-oriented, not treatment instructions.

### Phase 5: Claim and graph objects

Only after the bridge files are reviewed should any machine-readable claim or graph relationship objects be created.

Claim and graph objects must preserve:

- asserted vs inferred status;
- theological vs clinical scope;
- reviewer role;
- evidence basis;
- confidence and caution flags;
- crisis and referral boundaries.

### Phase 6: Contributor launch

Only after safety, vocabulary, front door, and bridge examples exist should outside Christian counselors be invited to contribute.

Invite them into narrow tasks:

- review safety language;
- improve role boundaries;
- identify missing vocabulary;
- review a bridge file;
- flag clinical safety concerns;
- propose safe educational resources.

Do not invite open-ended counseling models, case content, or treatment protocols.

## Safety guardrails for any future branch

Any future counseling branch must preserve these guardrails:

1. No client-identifiable material.
2. No therapy notes or psychotherapy notes.
3. No diagnosis or treatment plans.
4. No suicide, abuse, psychosis, substance-crisis, or self-harm workflows without licensed professional and crisis-policy review.
5. No AI system presented as counselor, therapist, pastor, spiritual director, or crisis responder.
6. No replacement of licensed care, pastoral accountability, emergency services, or mandated reporting.
7. No claim that a theological practice is a clinical intervention unless reviewed and scoped.
8. No spiritualized blame for mental illness, trauma, abuse, or suffering.
9. No unreviewed deliverance, inner-healing, prayer, confession, forgiveness, or repentance content as treatment guidance.
10. No movement from incoming research into application bridges without explicit review status.
11. No movement from bridges into claims or graph objects without qualified review.
12. No clinical-adjacent rendering without caution labels and role boundaries.

## Activation criteria

Do not activate this branch until at least three of the following are true:

- a Christian counselor or clinically trained reviewer is available;
- a pastoral-care reviewer is available;
- a theologian or biblical scholar is available;
- the AI-governance bridge pattern is already established;
- a safety-policy draft is ready;
- the contributor front door is stable;
- there is a specific use case that is educational or governance-oriented rather than clinical treatment.

## Recommended first activation task

If activated later, the first task should be a safety and role-boundary seed, not a counseling content pack.

The first output should answer:

- who can contribute;
- what they can contribute;
- what must never be contributed;
- where staged material lives;
- what review is required;
- what safety boundaries are non-negotiable.

- client-note ingestion;
- clinical advice;
