# radah

## Why this term matters

`radah` matters because it appears in Genesis 1 in direct connection with humanity’s role in creation. It therefore sits close to Christian reasoning about stewardship, authority, vocation, and the moral limits of delegated rule.

For this project, that makes it highly relevant not only to doctrine, but also to governance, institutions, and AI-era questions about delegated agency and the proper use of power.

## Primary passages

The central first-order passages are Genesis 1:26 and 1:28.

## Basic lexical note

The term is often translated with language such as “have dominion” or “rule.” Those glosses are not wrong, but they can become misleading if they are detached from the theological context of creaturehood, accountability, and ordered creation.

In this repository, `radah` should not be read as self-authorized domination. It should be read within a wider theological logic of delegated responsibility.

## Why it is translation-sensitive

The term is translation-sensitive because modern readers may hear “dominion” as exploitative mastery, while some translations or target languages may soften it so much that authority nearly disappears.

This means the lexical and doctrinal work needs to preserve both:
- real authority
- creaturely limit and accountability

## Relation to doctrine and governance

`radah` is especially relevant to:
- stewardship
- authority
- vocation
- governance as delegated rather than sovereign rule
- institutional power under moral limit
- AI and automation questions where delegation can be confused with abdication or domination

## Interpretive caution

This repository should avoid:
- turning `radah` into a warrant for exploitative control
- flattening the term into morally neutral administration
- detaching authority from theology of creation, creaturehood, and accountability
