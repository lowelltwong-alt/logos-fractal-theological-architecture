# tselem

## Why this term matters

`tselem` is one of the key lexical nodes for the project because it sits directly inside Genesis 1:26–28 and therefore inside any serious Christian account of the human person, dignity, vocation, stewardship, and authority.

For this repository, the term matters because anthropology is upstream of AI governance. If the human person is image-bearing in a thick theological sense, then governance cannot treat persons as merely reducible to outputs, scores, or machine-legible profiles.

## Primary passage

The central first-order passage is Genesis 1:26–28, where humanity is described using `tselem` together with `demut`.

## Basic lexical note

The standard gloss is usually “image,” but that English rendering can be too thin if readers assume only visual resemblance or static representation.

Within the theological architecture of this repository, `tselem` should be treated as a term requiring doctrinal and contextual interpretation rather than a self-explanatory English equivalent.

## Why it is translation-sensitive

The term is translation-sensitive because:
- English "image" can sound flatter than the theological freight the term carries
- different modern languages may preserve slightly different emphases
- the doctrinal importance of the term is shaped by book context, canonical context, and theological tradition

This means the repository should not treat an English gloss alone as sufficient lexical analysis.

## Cross-language note

Useful renderings may appear in multiple modern languages, but no single target-language gloss should be assumed to exhaust the semantic range.

For example:
- English often uses “image”
- Chinese often uses 形像
- Russian often uses образ

These may overlap strongly while still carrying different associative pressures in theological and cultural use.

## Relation to doctrine and governance

`tselem` is especially relevant to:
- concept.imago_dei
- theological anthropology
- dignity and irreducibility of the person
- delegated authority and stewardship
- limits on reductive AI categorization

## Interpretive caution

This repository should avoid reducing `tselem` to:
- mere physical resemblance
- a minimal abstract status term with no implications for vocation
- a concept detachable from Genesis 1’s wider account of humanity, dominion, and created order
