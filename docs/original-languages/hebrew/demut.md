# demut

## Why this term matters

`demut` matters because it is paired with `tselem` in Genesis 1:26 and therefore sits near the center of Christian reflection on the image of God.

For this project, it is important because traditions have often handled the image/likeness relationship differently, and those differences can flow downstream into anthropology, sanctification, participation, and theological accounts of human vocation.

## Primary passage

The central first-order passage is Genesis 1:26, where `demut` appears alongside `tselem`.

## Basic lexical note

The usual gloss is “likeness,” but the theological significance of the term depends heavily on how it is read in relation to `tselem`.

Some readings emphasize similarity or analogy. Others use the pair to build stronger anthropological distinctions. The repository should preserve those interpretive differences rather than collapsing them too early.

## Why it is translation-sensitive

The term is translation-sensitive because “likeness” can sound vague or secondary in English, while some theological traditions have given it distinct weight.

This means lexical work should not stop at gloss level.

## Relation to doctrine and governance

`demut` is especially relevant to:
- concept.imago_dei
- anthropology
- image and likeness debates across traditions
- sanctification, transformation, and participation questions
- the depth of human dignity and representational responsibility

## Interpretive caution

This repository should avoid two opposite mistakes:
- treating `demut` as though it adds nothing at all
- building overconfident doctrinal systems from a lexical distinction without adequate contextual and tradition-level support
