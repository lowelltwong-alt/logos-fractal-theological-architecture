# Anthropology (Legacy Path)

> Canonical file: `docs/doctrine/anthropology.md`.
> Keep this legacy copy for compatibility and avoid introducing new doctrine changes here.

## Purpose

This doctrine node explains how the Logos project understands the human person and why anthropology is one of the most important theological control points for AI governance, institutional design, and LAIRCA-style decision systems.

Anthropology matters because many modern systems quietly assume an answer to the question of what a person is before any explicit moral reasoning begins. If that assumption is thin, mechanistic, or purely functional, the rest of the architecture will drift in the same direction.

## Summary

In the Christian theological vision assumed by this repository, the human person is not merely a biological organism, an economic unit, a preference bundle, a role-bearer, or an information pattern. The human person is a morally significant creature made by God, bearing dignity that is not self-generated, called into accountable agency, wounded by sin, and not reducible to a score, prediction, or optimization target.

This anthropology has strong implications for any institution using AI, ranking systems, process automation, governance workflows, or decision-support tools.

## Why anthropology is a first-order doctrine in this project

Anthropology is treated as first-order here because it governs several downstream questions immediately:

- what counts as a person versus an object of management
- how far delegated judgment may go
- what kind of oversight remains necessary
- whether efficiency can override dignity
- whether ranking systems are describing risk, assigning worth, or hardening into hidden moral judgments
- whether responsibility belongs to a human agent, a process, or an institution

If anthropology is weak, the architecture will tend to become technocratic even if the language remains Christian.

## Key theological affirmations

### Humans possess inherent dignity
Human dignity is not granted by usefulness, institutional status, moral performance, social recognition, or system output. It is intrinsic to the human person and should therefore function as an upstream constraint on governance design.

### Humans are accountable agents
Persons are not only acted upon. They are agents who bear responsibility, make judgments, receive obligations, and can answer for their actions. A healthy decision architecture should preserve rather than erase that answerability.

### Humans are relational beings
Persons exist in relation to God and neighbor. That means moral reasoning cannot be reduced to isolated individual preference or pure system optimization. Community, obligation, and neighbor-love are part of the architecture.

### Humans are finite
Persons are not omniscient, not perfectly rational, and not endlessly scalable. This supports the need for prudence, distributed accountability, and caution in system design.

### Humans are fallen
Because persons and institutions are touched by sin, neither human judgment nor machine-assisted processes can be treated as neutral. Anthropology therefore links directly to governance safeguards, review structures, and humility about any system’s claims.

## Internal tensions to preserve

A strong doctrine node should not hide tensions. The following tensions should remain visible:

### Dignity and corruption
Humans possess dignity, but they are also fallen. Governance must therefore avoid both romanticizing human judgment and replacing it too quickly with technical systems.

### Agency and dependence
Humans are real agents, but not sovereign. Systems should preserve agency while resisting the fantasy that any actor can operate free of moral order or accountability.

### Individual responsibility and institutional structure
Persons matter, but persons always act inside institutional settings. A mature framework should therefore preserve individual answerability without ignoring structural pressures, incentives, and power.

### Use of tools and danger of reduction
Tools can extend human capacities, but they can also tempt institutions to redefine people according to what the tool can measure.

## Governance implications

A serious Christian anthropology implies the following governance requirements:

1. consequential decisions should retain identifiable human ownership
2. persons should not be treated as merely ranked units in morally significant contexts
3. appeal, review, and explanation become more important when systems influence outcomes affecting dignity
4. institutions should avoid hiding moral judgment inside technical categories
5. system design should distinguish assistance from replacement
6. policies should guard against depersonalization, coercion, and unchallengeable classifications

## AI and decision-system implications

Anthropology directly shapes the use of AI in at least six ways.

### 1. Limits on reduction
AI systems often work by simplification. That is useful, but it becomes dangerous when simplification hardens into an account of what the person is. The architecture must resist any move from “useful representation” to “complete definition.”

### 2. Limits on ranking
Ranking may be appropriate for low-consequence operational tasks, but in morally significant settings ranking can drift into hidden judgment about value, trustworthiness, or worth. Anthropology requires caution here.

### 3. Need for accountable review
If persons are not reducible to a model output, then consequential judgments affecting persons should not rely solely on machine outputs. Human review is not a cosmetic control. It is anthropologically necessary.

### 4. Boundaries on automation
Not every act of decision should be operationalized into autonomous execution. Where dignity, vocation, discipline, exclusion, care, or moral interpretation are at stake, stronger human responsibility is required.

### 5. Attention to formation
Systems do not only process decisions. They shape people. A Christian anthropology should therefore ask what kinds of users, leaders, or institutions an AI-enabled workflow is forming.

### 6. Caution about invisibility
The more invisible a system becomes, the easier it is for institutions to deny where moral agency has actually shifted. Anthropology requires that responsibility remain visible.

## Relationship to ordering

Anthropology appears early in the default ordering because optimization must not define the person. The doctrine of the person must come before the design of the system.

## Relationship to weighting

Anthropology strongly supports giving very high weight to:
- dignity
- accountability
- protection against coercion
- meaningful human review

It supports assigning only moderate or conditional weight to:
- efficiency
- aggressive personalization
- autonomous ranking of persons

## Downstream derivation path

This doctrine should directly feed the following derivation chain:

`anthropology -> dignity and responsibility ethics -> governance constraints on AI use -> LAIRCA configuration -> institutional review workflow`
