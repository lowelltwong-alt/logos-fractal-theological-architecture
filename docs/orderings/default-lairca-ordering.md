# Default LAIRCA Ordering

## Purpose

This document provides a full working default ordering profile for using the Logos project as the theological upstream architecture for LAIRCA-style decision systems.

It is intended to be broad enough for serious Christian reuse while still being explicit enough to constrain downstream governance and AI-assisted reasoning.

## Core claim

A Christian decision architecture should not begin with capability, data availability, process speed, or model performance.
It should begin with reality as ordered under Logos, then move through theological and moral constraints before reaching design, automation, and optimization.

## Full default order

1. Logos and truth
2. doctrine of God
3. Christology
4. revelation and Scripture
5. anthropology and human dignity
6. sin and fallenness
7. wisdom, prudence, and moral reasoning
8. justice, mercy, and neighbor-love
9. vocation, stewardship, and responsibility
10. authority, community, and institutional ordering
11. technology, tools, and mediation
12. application-specific optimization

## Explanation of each stage

### 1. Logos and truth
Reality is not self-generated by the institution and is not defined by what is most efficient to compute. The ordering begins with Logos because the project assumes that truth, coherence, intelligibility, and moral order are prior to all systems. This first stage prevents the framework from collapsing into instrumentalism.

Practical consequence:
- systems must be evaluated for truthfulness, not only usefulness
- machine output cannot be treated as self-validating
- institutional narratives cannot redefine reality at will

### 2. Doctrine of God
The character of God constrains what the system may treat as good, just, wise, or faithful. Governance is not ultimately grounded in organizational preference. It is answerable to a prior moral and metaphysical order.

Practical consequence:
- ultimate standards cannot be reduced to local KPIs
- power must be judged morally, not only operationally
- theological claims about order, justice, and responsibility shape the framework early

### 3. Christology
Christology matters because Christian judgment is not abstract monotheism. It is shaped by the person and work of Christ, which affects how authority, sacrifice, service, truth, and reconciliation are understood.

Practical consequence:
- institutional design should not idolize domination or pure control
- leadership models should be tested against Christ-shaped authority
- mission and moral witness remain relevant to system design

### 4. Revelation and Scripture
The framework places revelation early so that interpretation remains accountable to a source outside the institution and outside the model. Scripture does not eliminate prudence, but it does place boundaries around what counts as faithful reasoning.

Practical consequence:
- Christian decision systems need authoritative source anchoring
- practical innovation must remain reviewable against revealed norms
- the framework should preserve citation, lineage, and interpretive transparency

### 5. Anthropology and human dignity
This is one of the most consequential points for AI governance. Human beings are not only data points, labor units, or optimization targets. They are morally significant persons whose dignity and agency place limits on what may be delegated, ranked, predicted, or automated.

Practical consequence:
- human oversight is not merely a compliance feature
- personhood must outrank convenience in consequential settings
- decisions affecting dignity, calling, or moral standing require strong human accountability

### 6. Sin and fallenness
A sound Christian architecture cannot skip sin. Human beings, institutions, and tools are all vulnerable to corruption, self-deception, domination, and distorted incentives. This stage prevents naive trust in both leaders and systems.

Practical consequence:
- governance must assume misuse is possible
- AI systems should be audited for bias, concealment, and incentive distortion
- institutions should distrust their own self-justifying narratives

### 7. Wisdom, prudence, and moral reasoning
Even true principles can be badly applied. Prudence is the stage that asks what is fitting here, now, for these people, under these constraints. It prevents the architecture from becoming a rigid machine that mistakes rule possession for judgment.

Practical consequence:
- the system should not flatten every decision into universal automation
- escalation logic matters
- thresholds, exceptions, and review processes are signs of maturity, not weakness

### 8. Justice, mercy, and neighbor-love
Justice and mercy must both appear here because the project assumes moral reasoning is relational and not merely formal. Neighbor-love is not sentimental add-on language. It helps determine what kinds of harms, burdens, and exclusions an institution should refuse to normalize.

Practical consequence:
- systems should be examined for who bears the hidden cost
- procedural consistency is insufficient by itself
- vulnerable persons should not be sacrificed to administrative neatness

### 9. Vocation, stewardship, and responsibility
Now the framework turns toward entrusted action. Institutions have real work to do, and stewardship matters. But stewardship appears after truth, dignity, sin, prudence, and justice so that responsibility is not quietly equated with raw throughput.

Practical consequence:
- efficiency is permitted but subordinated
- role clarity matters
- human responsibility should remain visible at every consequential layer

### 10. Authority, community, and institutional ordering
Only after the prior layers are established should formal authority and organizational structure take center stage. This prevents the institution from treating itself as the source of moral legitimacy.

Practical consequence:
- authority should remain accountable and reviewable
- governance architecture should show who can decide, who must review, and where appeal is possible
- community obligations should not be erased by purely top-down design

### 11. Technology, tools, and mediation
Technology enters late because tools are not ultimate. They mediate action but should not define the good. A tool may be impressive and still be wrongly placed in the architecture.

Practical consequence:
- choose tools after clarifying moral and governance logic
- do not automate what should first be judged humanly
- model capability should not set the ontology

### 12. Application-specific optimization
Optimization is last on purpose. It is real, useful, and often necessary, but it belongs at the end of the chain. Once it moves upward, it begins to deform the architecture.

Practical consequence:
- optimization should always be tested against upstream constraints
- a gain in speed does not justify a loss in dignity or accountability
- local metrics should never become hidden first principles

## Decision test derived from this ordering

Before implementing any AI-enabled workflow, governance model, or institutional process, ask:

1. Is it truthful?
2. Is it compatible with the character of God and the lordship of Christ?
3. Is it accountable to revealed norms?
4. Does it preserve human dignity and agency?
5. Does it take sin and institutional corruption seriously?
6. Has prudence been exercised rather than bypassed?
7. Does it serve justice, mercy, and neighbor-love?
8. Does it preserve visible human responsibility?
9. Is authority properly bounded and reviewable?
10. Is the tool serving the mission rather than defining it?
11. Is optimization staying in its proper place?

## Typical failure modes this ordering is designed to prevent

- capability-first governance
- efficiency-first institutional design
- black-box ranking in morally significant contexts
- automation that displaces accountability
- reduction of persons to metrics or risk scores
- institutional self-justification hidden under technical language

## Appropriate use cases

This ordering is especially suitable for:
- Christian AI ethics frameworks
- nonprofit and ministry governance
- institutionally accountable decision-support systems
- leadership models that require both structure and moral seriousness

## Limits

This profile is a working default, not a final dogmatic statement. Other traditions may affirm the same theological core while adjusting practical order in meaningful ways. Those differences should be surfaced through comparative profiles rather than concealed.
