# LAIRCA

LAIRCA is the Logos-grounded Christian instantiation layer that sits upstream of operational AIRCA-style decision systems.

## Purpose

This branch explains how Christian theological commitments configure a decision architecture rather than merely decorate it.

In this repository, LAIRCA is not treated as a slogan. It is treated as a structured way of asking how Logos-grounded theology changes the meaning of Architect, Inform, Rank, Commit, and Act.

## Working interpretation

For this repository, a practical working interpretation is:

- **L** = Logos grounding and theological source layer
- **A** = Architect
- **I** = Inform
- **R** = Rank
- **C** = Commit
- **A** = Act

The L-layer is what distinguishes LAIRCA from a generic decision framework. It supplies the metaphysical, theological, anthropological, and moral assumptions that constrain everything downstream.

## Why LAIRCA is necessary

AIRCA provides a powerful operational structure for decision systems.
But the same operational structure can be instantiated under very different visions of reality, personhood, value, responsibility, and authority.

LAIRCA names the specifically Christian upstream configuration of that architecture.

This matters because no decision system is actually neutral. Every system has a background account of:
- what persons are
- what counts as truth
- what goods matter
- what risks are tolerable
- who may decide
- what may be delegated
- what kind of action is faithful or justified

LAIRCA makes those assumptions explicit and theological.

## The role of the L-layer

The L-layer does not merely add Christian language. It configures the rest of the framework.

It answers questions like:
- What is reality understood to be?
- What is a person?
- What is the moral status of truth?
- What are the limits of delegated agency?
- What must remain accountable to human judgment?
- What counts as wisdom rather than mere optimization?

## How the theological layer reshapes each stage

### Architect
Architect is where goals, constraints, roles, system boundaries, and success criteria are defined.

Under LAIRCA, Architect must be shaped by theological convictions before efficiency goals are finalized.

Key implications:
- goals must be tested against truth, dignity, vocation, and moral order
- system boundaries must respect human responsibility and institutional accountability
- success criteria cannot be reduced to throughput or friction removal
- governance must be designed before aggressive automation

### Inform
Inform is where signals, evidence, context, options, and explanations are surfaced.

Under LAIRCA, Inform is not morally neutral. The question is not only what information can be surfaced, but what should count as relevant, interpretable, and morally admissible.

Key implications:
- information should not be selected solely for predictive power
- truthfulness outranks persuasive fluency
- context about uncertainty, limitation, and provenance matters
- data should not erase personhood by over-objectifying the subject

### Rank
Rank is where alternatives, risks, priorities, or options are ordered.

Under LAIRCA, ranking must remain accountable to moral order rather than becoming a hidden utilitarian engine.

Key implications:
- persons should not be ranked in ways that imply reducible worth
- tradeoffs must be reviewable and explainable
- higher goods should not be silently sacrificed to lower efficiencies
- uncertainty should often increase caution rather than create false precision

### Commit
Commit is the hinge of responsibility.
It is where someone owns the judgment and accepts answerability for the action taken.

Under LAIRCA, Commit is especially important because Christian governance does not permit responsibility to dissolve into process.

Key implications:
- consequential decisions should retain named human ownership
- review and escalation should be visible
- accountability should not be hidden behind technical systems
- institutions should be able to explain why they decided, not only what they decided

### Act
Act is where decisions are executed.

Under LAIRCA, execution is legitimate only when it remains downstream of theological, moral, and governance constraints. Action is not justified merely because it is operationally possible.

Key implications:
- low-consequence routine actions may be more safely automated
- consequential actions require stronger oversight and explicit bounds
- the act layer must preserve auditability and appeal where needed
- execution should serve the mission rather than redefine it

## Core theological pressures LAIRCA places on the architecture

1. truth before persuasion
2. dignity before throughput
3. prudence before scale
4. accountability before frictionless automation
5. stewardship without technocratic idolatry
6. service without depersonalization

## What LAIRCA does not imply

LAIRCA does not imply that every decision must be slow, manual, or suspicious of all tools.
It does imply that tools must serve a prior moral and theological order.

LAIRCA also does not imply that all Christian traditions will instantiate the framework in exactly the same way. Different traditions may vary in emphasis, ordering, or weighting while remaining recognizably Christian.
