## Theological source claim set

### Direct theological claims carried into this bridge

- Human persons, institutions, and tools are vulnerable to distortion and cannot be assumed morally neutral.
- Fallenness means drift, rationalization, and hidden incentive pressure should be expected rather than treated as rare anomalies.
- The repository should not let repeated exception signals silently rewrite higher-trust truth claims.

### Prudential governance applications derived from those claims

- Auditability and review paths should be treated as part of theological realism, not merely compliance overhead.
- Recurring exception patterns may indicate hidden harm or category drift and should trigger human review.
- AI-generated or inferred material should not silently auto-promote into doctrine, governance, or canonical source layers.

## Governance implications

- High-impact AI workflows should preserve audit trails and review triggers.
- Category systems should be examined for moral drift rather than treated as neutral just because they are operational.
- Exception signals may support proposals, but governed review should remain the gate for stronger promotion.
- AI safety in this repository should include institutional and moral drift, not only technical model error.

## What the bridge permits

- Using exception patterns as review signals.
- Building audit and escalation controls downstream of theological anthropology and fallenness.
- Extending safety review into institutional incentives, misuse, and hidden self-protection.
- Later claim-object or policy work after this prose bridge is reviewed.

## What the bridge forbids

- Treating AI safety as only a technical robustness problem.
- Allowing category hardening to become hidden moral judgment without review.
- Using exception signals to silently mutate doctrine, governance, or canonical source material.
- Presenting this bridge as if it were itself final doctrine or settled policy.

## Tradition-scope caution

This bridge assumes broad Christian agreement that distortion, misuse, and pride are real governance risks.

Specific doctrinal framing of sin, moral disorder, or institutional corruption may still need tradition-specific refinement in later work.
