# Default AI Governance Weighting

## Purpose

This document provides a full starter weighting profile for Christian AI governance use cases derived from the Logos project.

It is meant to guide institutions that want to use AI seriously without letting machine capability silently outrank truth, dignity, prudence, or responsibility.

## Governing principle

The question is not whether AI can increase efficiency.
The question is whether efficiency is being pursued under a right moral order.

This profile therefore gives the highest practical force to considerations that protect truth, personhood, accountability, and moral limits, while assigning lower or conditional force to considerations that mainly express speed, convenience, or optimization.

## Weighting model

### Hard constraints
These are non-negotiable boundaries. A system should fail review if it violates them.

- intentional deception
- coercive manipulation
- denial or erosion of human moral standing
- removal of accountable human review in morally consequential decisions
- concealment of material system limitations where people are likely to rely on the output
- use of AI to obscure ownership of judgment or responsibility

### Very high weight
These considerations should normally override convenience or local optimization.

- truthfulness
- imago Dei and human dignity
- human responsibility and answerability
- protection against coercion, domination, and exploitation
- preservation of meaningful human review in consequential contexts
- moral limits on means, not only on ends

### High weight
These carry strong force and should shape governance architecture directly.

- prudence
- justice
- stewardship
- transparency of lineage, process, and review
- clarity about where the model is assisting versus deciding
- proportionality between tool power and oversight intensity

### Moderate weight
These matter but should usually remain downstream of higher commitments.

- consistency
- timeliness
- process simplicity
- selective automation of routine work
- administrative efficiency
- scale where upstream safeguards are already established

### Conditional weight
These may be helpful in restricted settings, but should not be treated as governing values.

- personalization
- predictive ranking of persons in morally significant settings
- autonomous execution beyond low-consequence tasks
- black-box optimization of sensitive choices
- aggressive friction removal where friction is actually protecting responsibility

## Why these weights are ordered this way

### Truthfulness outranks convenience
AI systems can produce fluent error, selective framing, or plausible nonsense. If truth is not given dominant force, the institution will gradually confuse usefulness with reality. Christian governance cannot allow that substitution.

### Dignity outranks throughput
Once persons are reduced to inputs, scores, or workflow obstacles, the architecture is already morally damaged. A Christian system must actively resist depersonalization, especially where AI makes depersonalization administratively attractive.

### Accountability outranks frictionless automation
Many AI systems appear valuable precisely because they dissolve visible responsibility. This profile rejects that dynamic. A good system should preserve the ability to say who judged, who approved, and who bears responsibility for the consequence.

### Prudence outranks blind scale
Even beneficial tools can be misapplied when rolled out too quickly or too broadly. Prudence demands that capability be governed by fit, not merely by availability.

### Stewardship matters, but is not ultimate
Stewardship supports efficiency, wise use of time, and reduction of waste. But stewardship becomes distorted when it is used to justify systems that compromise dignity, obscure truth, or weaken responsibility.

## Practical review questions

Before approving an AI-enabled workflow, ask:

1. Does the system preserve truthfulness rather than merely persuasive output?
2. Does it treat people as persons rather than manageable objects?
3. Is there a named accountable human for consequential outcomes?
4. Are the limits of the system visible to the people relying on it?
5. Is the level of oversight proportional to the seriousness of the decision?
6. Would this workflow still be defended if efficiency gains were modest?
7. Is automation being used where it serves stewardship, or where it hides responsibility?

## Typical use cases where this profile fits

- AI-assisted policy drafting
- decision-support systems in nonprofit or ministry settings
- summarization and triage systems with human review
- knowledge retrieval and synthesis under clear governance
- early institutional experimentation with AI where moral caution is warranted

## Use cases where stronger restrictions may be needed

- personnel decisions affecting dignity or livelihood
- decisions involving discipline, exclusion, or moral standing
- high-stakes counseling or pastoral settings
- allocation of scarce aid or services to vulnerable persons
- any system that pressures users to trust outputs they cannot meaningfully challenge

## Common failure modes this profile is trying to prevent

- model fluency being mistaken for truth
- efficiency being treated as moral justification
- humans remaining nominally in the loop but functionally displaced
- lack of review because the system appears competent
- personalization becoming manipulation
- ranking systems hardening into hidden judgments about worth or risk

## Relationship to ordering

This weighting profile assumes the default LAIRCA ordering in which Logos, revelation, anthropology, sin, prudence, justice, stewardship, and institutional structure all appear before optimization.

If the ordering changes, the meaning of these weights may also change. Weightings should therefore always be interpreted alongside their parent ordering profile.
